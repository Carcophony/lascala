① The Big Idea
One Shooter.
One Ladder. One Goal.

Forget the sum of the dice — we don't care about that. In LaScala, what matters is the difference. The gap between the two numbers. That one small twist turns a familiar thing into something completely new.

Someone takes the role of Dice Shooter. They start somewhere on a numbered ladder and throw the dice, over and over, trying to climb to the top. Everyone else bets on whether they'll make it — or crash out.

The shooter starts at position 5 (their choice). They need to reach position 10 exactly.

On every throw, they move up by whatever the dice show as their difference. If they overshoot the top, they bounce back down.

If they roll a double at any point — game over. They failed. The game can end on any single throw. That's the tension.

② The Dice
It's the Difference That Counts

The difference can only ever be 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, or 5. And here's the thing — zero means doubles. Both dice the same number. That ends the game instantly. No move. Game over.

This happens 1 in 6 throws. Every single throw, there's a one-in-six chance of sudden death. That's what makes every throw electric.

③ How to Play
Step by Step
1

Pick a Shooter

One player is the Dice Shooter for the round. Everyone else bets against or alongside them. The shooter role rotates each game so everyone gets a turn.

2

Choose a Start Position

The shooter picks where to start on the ladder. Start low (closer to 1) for a longer, more dramatic game with bigger odds. Start high (closer to the top) for a quick game with lower odds.

3

Place Your Bets

Before each throw, everyone bets. You can bet that the shooter will Make It to the top, that they Won't Make It, or you can bet on exactly what the next throw's difference will be (0–5). You can do all of these.

4

Throw the Dice

Shooter throws two dice. The difference determines the move. Throw bets pay out immediately. Make It / Don't Make It bets stay active until the game ends.

5

Move the Shooter (or End the Game)

If it's a double (zero): game over, shooter loses. If they land exactly on top: shooter wins. If they overshoot, they bounce back. Otherwise: bet again, throw again.

The Throw Bets have fixed odds — they never change because the dice don't change. Diff 1 (the most common) pays less. Diff 5 (rare) pays a lot more. The odds always reflect the actual probability of each outcome.

The Game Bets change on every throw — as the shooter gets closer to the top, "Make It" odds shorten. As they get further away, they lengthen. You can see the market shifting in real time and bet accordingly.

⑤ The Bounce Rule
Too Far? Bounce Back.

This is the rule that causes the most drama and confusion — but it's actually very simple.

If the shooter would land above the top rung, they don't reach the top. Instead, they bounce back by the excess. Think of the top as a wall they bounce off.

EXAMPLE — BOUNCE IN ACTION
🪜 Ladder top: 10
🎲 Shooter is at: 8
🎲 Dice show: 3 and 6 → difference = 3
➕ 8 + 3 = 11 (overshoots by 1)
🔁 New position: 10 − 1 = 9 (bounced back!)
😬 Shooter now needs exactly 1 to win.

The bounce rule means that being close to the top is not the same as being safe. Near the top, you can be thrown back down by a big throw. And needing exactly 1 is actually quite hard — it only happens on 10 out of 36 throws.

⑥ The Advanced Move
The Guaranteed Return

This is the feature that makes LaScala genuinely unique among betting games. Because the odds on Make It and Don't Make It change during play, a smart player can sometimes bet both sides and guarantee a profit regardless of outcome.

You don't need to understand the maths for this. Just know it's possible, and watch for the opportunity.

1

Early in the game — shooter is far from top

Make It odds are long (say 32/10). You bet £100 on Make It. If they win, you get back £420 total.

2

Game progresses — shooter is near the top

Make It odds have shortened dramatically (now 11/10). Don't Make It odds have lengthened. You bet £200 on Don't Make It. If they fail, you get back £420 total.

✓

The guaranteed outcome

You've staked £300 total. You collect £420 no matter what happens. Locked-in profit of £120.

Total Staked: £300  ·  Guaranteed Return: £420  ·  Profit: +£120 whatever happens

This is real mathematics, not a trick. It only works when the odds shift enough during the game. When it does come off, it feels brilliant. You bet both sides and can't lose. That's LaScala's secret weapon.

⑦ Common Questions
You Asked
Can you bet on every throw?
Yes. Before every single throw, you can place fresh bets on both the throw outcome (Diff 0–5) and the overall game (Make It / Don't Make It). Your previous game bets stay active — you're just adding to them if you want.
Can only the shooter throw the dice?
Yes — the Dice Shooter is the designated player for that round. Everyone else is betting on them. The role rotates after each game so everyone gets to shoot.
What happens if the shooter rolls 0, 0, 0, 0... and never moves?
Doubles end the game immediately. So if the shooter rolls a double on the very first throw, the game is over in one roll. Don't Make It bets win, Make It bets lose. It's brutal. It's also why the tension is real from throw one.
How long does a game last?
It entirely depends. A shooter can be wiped out on the first throw (if they roll a double). Or they can have an epic run of 15+ throws on a long ladder. The average game on a 10-rung ladder from position 5 is roughly 3–5 throws — but the variance is huge, which is the whole point.
What's the best starting position?
There's no "best" position — it's a strategic choice. Starting high gives shorter odds on Make It (less to win) but a shorter game. Starting low gives longer odds (bigger wins if they make it) but more throws and more chances to hit a double. Shrewd bettors will adjust their Make It / Don't Make It bets based on the start position.
Can I bet against the shooter if I'm the shooter?
Technically possible, but where's the fun in that? House rules apply — in most casual games, the shooter is expected to bet on themselves making it. But for a friendly game, you make the rules.
Do I need real money to play?
Not at all. The digital version uses fictional chips. For a physical game at home, you can use poker chips, pennies, matchsticks, or whatever you like. The game works just as well as a social betting game without real money involved.
